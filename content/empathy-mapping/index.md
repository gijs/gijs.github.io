We ran a half-day empathy mapping session on March 5th, and it produced the most useful single artefact we've made in months. It now lives on a wall in the office and gets referenced in almost every product conversation.

The session started in the physical world — a table covered in sticky notes, coffee, and the particular kind of productive chaos that happens when five people who rarely sit together start externalising their assumptions about a user. We had two product people, two developers, and one person from the water authority side who knows the actual users well.

## The empathy map

We use the standard six-section format: what the user **thinks**, **feels**, **hears**, **says**, **does**, and **sees**. For a water management professional this turns out to be a rich canvas.

The *thinks* quadrant filled up quickly. Water professionals think about risk constantly — not in an anxious way, but as a professional habit. They're always doing informal probability estimates: what are the odds this dike section holds? What happens downstream if we make this call wrong? They also think a lot about accountability, because decisions about water levels affect farmers, homeowners, and ecosystems, and there are always stakeholders ready to question those decisions after the fact.

The *does* quadrant was revealing in a different way. The actual daily workflow involves a surprising amount of Excel. Not because these professionals lack technical sophistication — many have deep backgrounds in hydraulics modelling — but because Excel is the lingua franca that everyone in the chain can open and understand. Any tool we build that can't export to something Excel-compatible is effectively invisible to a large part of the workflow.

## What we learned

The biggest surprise was in the *hears* section. We'd assumed that the main pressure was from regulators and water management legislation. That's there, but it's background noise. The louder signal is from colleagues and adjacent teams: the person managing the neighbouring polder, the municipality that wants to build on a flood-prone plot, the farmer whose drainage is affected by level decisions upstream.

Rana currently models the technical side of water management well. It models the social and organisational side of it almost not at all. That gap is now clearly visible, and it's shaping several items on the discovery board.

## Running the session

A few things made this work: keeping it small (five people maximum), having someone with actual domain knowledge in the room rather than just inferring from interviews, and spending the last thirty minutes translating insights directly into "how might we" questions. The digital version in FigJam captures the output, but the physical session was where the thinking happened.
